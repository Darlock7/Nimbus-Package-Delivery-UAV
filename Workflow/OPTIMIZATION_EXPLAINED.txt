--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  1. WHAT ARE WE MAXIMIZING? THE SCORING FUNCTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The competition gives us this formula:

  J = (rWp*Wp + rVp*Vp - ce*Ef - cc) / Tf  -  (cWg*Wg)/tl  -  cf

Every term means something physical:

  Term           Coefficient              Meaning
  ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  rWp * Wp       $0.225 per Newton        Revenue from payload WEIGHT
  rVp * Vp       $300 per m³              Revenue from payload VOLUME
  -ce * Ef       -$1.39e-6 per Joule      ENERGY COST — penalizes fuel burn
  -cc            -$0.50                   Fixed cost per flight
  / Tf           divide by total time     Rewards flying FASTER
  -cWg*Wg/tl     -$22.5/N amortized       STRUCTURAL COST — heavier aircraft
                                           cost more over their life
  -cf            -$0.000111/s             Fixed time operating cost

The fundamental tension: to maximize J you want to carry heavy/large packages
(revenue up), burn little energy (cost down), fly fast (Tf down), and be as
light as possible (amortized cost down). These four goals fight each other
constantly. A heavier payload needs a bigger wing, which burns more energy.
A faster cruise speed requires more thrust, which burns more energy. The
optimizer's job is to find the combination where these tradeoffs balance out
at maximum profit.

In our case, the optimizer always pushes payload volume to the maximum (30 L)
because the revenue coefficient rVp = $300/m³ is large enough that more volume
always outweighs the weight penalty. This is an important insight — the scoring
function is telling us that volume matters more than weight for this competition.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  2. WHY THIS IS A HARD PROBLEM
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Three properties make this impossible to solve with a simple gradient-based
optimizer like MATLAB's fmincon:

NON-CONVEX LANDSCAPE
  There are multiple local optima. A low-AR wide wing can match profit with a
  high-AR narrow wing through completely different tradeoffs in L/D, stall
  speed, and structural mass. A gradient-based method gets trapped in whichever
  basin it starts in and never finds the global optimum.

NON-SMOOTH PHYSICS
  The profit function has discontinuities because:
  - AVL computes eigenvalues — stability mode classification (Level 1/2/3)
    creates step changes in the objective
  - Violated constraints return 1e6 immediately — a cliff, not a smooth slope
  - Airfoil surrogates are piecewise-interpolated — kinks at Re boundaries
  If you try to compute a numerical gradient near a constraint boundary, you
  get noise, not useful information. Gradient descent follows that noise off
  a cliff.

EXPENSIVE EVALUATIONS
  Each candidate design calls 6 physics models sequentially and runs the
  external AVL binary (~2 seconds per evaluation). With ~13,000 total
  evaluations, the optimizer runs 6-10 hours. You cannot afford to waste
  evaluations. The algorithm must extract maximum information from every
  function call.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  3. WHY CMA-ES — NOT GRADIENT DESCENT, NOT A GENETIC ALGORITHM
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

GRADIENT DESCENT (fmincon, Newton's method):
  Requires derivatives. Our objective is non-smooth and has hard constraint
  cliffs. Numerical gradients are unreliable and cause the optimizer to chase
  noise rather than find the optimum. Not suitable.

GENETIC ALGORITHMS:
  Robust and don't need gradients, but they are stateless — they build no
  internal model of the landscape. Each generation is essentially independent
  of prior history. For a 7-hour-per-run budget, you need something that
  learns from its history. Not efficient enough.

CMA-ES (Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy):
  Developed by Nikolaus Hansen at INRIA in 2001. It is specifically designed
  for the class of problems we have: expensive, non-convex, non-smooth, 5-20
  variables, no gradients available. It is the standard algorithm in aerospace
  multidisciplinary design optimization for exactly this reason.

  What makes it different: it LEARNS THE SHAPE of the search landscape as it
  runs. Specifically, it learns which directions in the 13-dimensional design
  space tend to produce better aircraft, and it biases future samples toward
  those directions. This is fundamentally more efficient than a genetic
  algorithm.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  4. HOW CMA-ES WORKS — STEP BY STEP
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

At every generation, CMA-ES maintains a multivariate Gaussian distribution
over the normalized design space. This distribution has three components:

  m  — mean vector: where the algorithm thinks the optimum currently is
  σ  — step size: how widely to sample around that mean
  C  — covariance matrix (13×13): the shape of the search — which directions
       in parameter space are promising

STEP 1 — SAMPLE lambda = 26 CANDIDATES:
  Z = randn(13, 26)            ← 26 random vectors from standard normal
  Y = eigenC * (diagD .* Z)    ← rotate and scale by covariance matrix C
  X = m + σ * Y                ← candidate designs in normalized [0,1] space

  The covariance matrix C determines the shape of the sample cloud.
  Early in the run, C = I (identity) so samples form a sphere.
  As learning proceeds, C elongates in the directions that produced
  good designs, forming an ellipsoid aligned with the profit landscape.

STEP 2 — EVALUATE ALL 26 CANDIDATES IN PARALLEL:
  Each candidate goes through the full physics pipeline (described in
  Section 6). Returns either a real profit J, or 1e6 if any constraint
  is violated. MATLAB's parfor runs these in parallel across CPU cores.

STEP 3 — RANK BY OBJECTIVE VALUE:
  Sort all 26 results from best to worst (lowest -J = highest profit).

STEP 4 — UPDATE THE MEAN TOWARD THE BEST HALF:
  m_new = sum( w_i * x_i )   for i = 1 to 13  (top half of candidates)

  Weights are log-spaced: the best candidate gets the most weight, the
  13th best gets the least. The mean shifts toward where good solutions
  were concentrated this generation.

STEP 5 — UPDATE THE COVARIANCE MATRIX C:
  This is the core innovation of CMA-ES. Two mechanisms work together:

  Rank-1 update (evolution path pc):
    Tracks the path the mean has moved over many consecutive generations.
    If the mean consistently moves in one direction (e.g., AR always
    increases when profit improves), C elongates along that direction
    so future samples explore it more.

  Rank-mu update:
    Directly uses all 13 top solutions from this generation to estimate
    which directions in parameter space produced good results. Updates
    C toward the observed distribution of successful candidates.

  After ~50 generations, if every good design had AR = 5-6 whenever
  sweep = 30-35 degrees, the C matrix develops a positive correlation
  between those two variables. Future samples automatically explore
  consistent AR-sweep combinations.

  This is fundamentally different from a genetic algorithm — CMA-ES
  builds a probabilistic model of the fitness landscape and uses it.

STEP 6 — ADAPT THE STEP SIZE σ (cumulative step length adaptation):
  Compare the cumulative path length |ps| to what a random walk would
  produce: chiN = sqrt(n) * (1 - 1/(4n) + 1/(21n²))

  If |ps| > chiN: mean is moving consistently in one direction
                  → steps are too small → σ increases
  If |ps| < chiN: mean is zigzagging (overshooting)
                  → steps are too large → σ decreases

  σ self-tunes with no user input required. The convergence plot
  (sigma vs. generation) shows this decay clearly.

STEP 7 — CHECK CONVERGENCE:
  Stop if any of the following:
  - σ < 1e-5: distribution has collapsed, no more progress possible
  - Best J changed by less than 1e-6 $/s over last 10 generations
  - 500 generations elapsed (maximum limit)


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  5. THE 13 DESIGN VARIABLES AND WHY EACH ONE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  #    Variable              Bounds           Why these bounds
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   1   Aspect Ratio AR       [4.0, 10.0]      Below 4: too much induced drag, hard to build
                                              Above 10: tip chord approaches zero

   2   Taper ratio           [0.50, 0.80]     Below 0.50: tip stalls before root (unsafe)
                                              Above 0.80: near-elliptic, diminishing returns

   3   Wing sweep (c/4)      [0.0, 35.0 deg]  Above 35: no transonic benefit at RC speeds;
                                              high sweep moves CG aft → pitch instability

   4   Root twist (washout)  [-3.0, -1.0 deg] FORCED NEGATIVE: tip must stall after root.
                                              Lower bound -1 deg: minimum 1 deg washout
                                              always enforced for stall protection.
                                              Upper bound -3 deg: more twist = more drag

   5   Wing loading W/S      [20, 90 N/m²]   Below 20: enormous wing, impractical to build
                                              Above 90: stall speed exceeds 12 m/s constraint

   6   xLE root position     [0.03, 0.30 m]  Controls CG location → controls static margin.
                                              Wing too far forward: high trim drag.
                                              Wing too far back: CG aft of NP → unstable.

   7   Vertical fin AR       [2.0, 2.5]      Originally 3.5, reduced because optimizer produced
                                              disproportionately tall winglets relative to
                                              wing tip chord.

   8   Vertical fin taper    [0.30, 0.70]    Manufacturing and structural practicality limits.

   9   Vertical fin sweep    [20, 45 deg]    Originally 60 deg, reduced for geometric
                                              proportionality. High sweep shifts fin
                                              aerodynamic center too far aft.

  10   Cruise speed          [20, 28 m/s]    Below 20: barely above stall (margin factor 1.3x)
                                              Above 28: propulsion system undersized for speed

  11   Payload volume Vp     [1, 30 L]       Upper bound = maximum that physically fits inside
                                              the fuselage airfoil cross-section.
                                              Optimizer always converges to 30 L.

  12   Fuselage half-width   [0.05, 0.20 m]  Physical size limit of fuselage construction

  13   Fuselage length       [0.60, 1.00 m]  Lower bound: must fit motor, battery, payload.
                                              Upper bound: 1 m reference design.

Variable 11 (payload volume) always converges to its upper bound of 30 L.
This tells you the revenue coefficient for volume ($300/m³) dominates the
structural cost penalty at any physically achievable fuselage size. The
scoring function is volume-dominated.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  6. WHAT HAPPENS INSIDE EACH EVALUATION — THE 19-STEP PIPELINE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

When the optimizer tests one candidate design, it runs this pipeline:

  Step  1   Decode 13 normalized [0,1] values → physical units
  Step  2   Estimate gross weight from empty-weight fraction model (fe model)
  Step  3   wingGeometryDesign → span, root chord, tip chord, MAC
  Step  4   CHECK: tip chord > 5 cm, total wingspan < 2.5 m    REJECT if violated
  Step  5   CHECK: payload volume fits inside fuselage airfoil  REJECT if violated
  Step  6   Evaluate root and tip airfoil surrogates at cruise Re
            (no XFOIL runtime call — uses pre-computed database)
  Step  7   twistFunctionPanknin → required spanwise washout distribution
  Step  8   verticalSurfaceDesign → winglet/fin geometry
  Step  9   CHECK: fin height < 50% semispan                    REJECT if violated
            CHECK: fin root chord < 1.2x wing tip chord         REJECT if violated
  Step 10   Scale wing and fin structural masses (physics-based scaling laws)
  Step 11   aircraftMassProperties → actual CG position and inertia tensor
  Step 12   aeroPolarAircraft → CD0, L/D, cruise CL and CD (drag build-up)
  Step 13   CHECK: stall speed Vs <= 12 m/s                     REJECT if violated
  Step 14   CHECK: takeoff ground roll <= 138 m (Raymer formula) REJECT if violated
  Step 15   CHECK: climb T/W feasible at V = 13 m/s             REJECT if violated
  Step 16   CHECK: turn T/W feasible at V = 13 m/s, n = 1.5    REJECT if violated
  Step 17   dynamicStabilityAVL → run AVL binary, parse eigenvalues,
            compute static margin, classify flight modes
  Step 18   Apply stability penalties (static margin band + modal damping)
  Step 19   energyCalc + profitPerUnitTime → compute J
            Return -J as the objective (CMA-ES minimizes, we want to maximize J)

Steps 1-16 take ~0.1 seconds total.
Step 17 (AVL) takes ~2 seconds and completely dominates runtime.
This is why the full optimization takes 6-10 hours.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  7. HARD CONSTRAINTS VS. SOFT PENALTIES — WHY THE DIFFERENCE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

HARD CONSTRAINTS (return 1e6 immediately, design rejected):

  These are binary — a design either works or it does not. Using a penalty
  here would be incorrect because there is no meaningful gradient pointing
  back toward feasibility from inside the infeasible region.

  Constraint                   Why it is hard
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Tip chord >= 5 cm            Below this you cannot physically build the wing
  Wingspan <= 2.5 m            Transportation limit — must fit in a car
  Stall speed <= 12 m/s        Competition rule — hard boundary, no partial credit
  Takeoff <= 138 m             Physical runway length
  Climb T/W feasible           Aircraft must be able to climb — binary requirement
  Turn T/W feasible            Aircraft must be able to turn at 1.5g — binary
  Payload fits in fuselage     Physical geometry — either it fits or it does not

SOFT PENALTIES (quadratic, added to the objective value):

  Static margin uses a soft quadratic penalty because AVL has numerical noise
  of +/-0.5% due to panel discretization. A hard cutoff at exactly 5% would
  cause discontinuous jumps that confuse the optimizer. The quadratic shape
  provides a smooth gradient guiding the optimizer toward the feasible region:

    SM < 5%:   penalty = 5.0 * (5 - SM)²    strongly pushes toward SM >= 5%
    SM > 13%:  penalty = 5.0 * (SM - 13)²   pushes toward SM <= 13%

  The coefficient 5.0 was chosen so that being 2% outside the band costs
  ~$0.50/hr of equivalent profit — enough to strongly discourage it without
  making it impossible to pass through during search.

DYNAMIC STABILITY FLAT PENALTIES:

  These use flat (step) penalties because modal damping has engineering
  thresholds from MIL-STD-1797 that are categorical, not continuous.

    Short-period zeta < 0:     +$0.50/hr   Divergent pitch — uncontrollable
    Short-period zeta < 0.25:  +$0.15/hr   Underdamped — difficult to control
    Phugoid zeta < 0:          +$0.10/hr   Unstable speed-altitude exchange
    Dutch roll zeta < 0:       +$0.15/hr   Unstable yaw-roll coupling
    Trim deflection > 15 deg:  +0.05 * (excess deg)   Excessive elevon trim drag

  The magnitudes are deliberately calibrated: the worst instability (divergent
  short period, +$0.50/hr) costs more than a significant fraction of total
  profit (~$23/hr), making it essentially prohibitive for the optimizer to
  accept such a design.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  8. THE KEY ENGINEERING TRADEOFFS THE OPTIMIZER RESOLVED
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

AR vs. structural weight:
  Pure aerodynamics wants AR = 8-9 (minimum induced drag, CD ∝ 1/AR).
  But higher AR increases wingspan and structural mass proportionally.
  The optimizer settled at AR ≈ 5.35 because the structural mass penalty
  in the profit function (cWg*Wg/tl) dominates once wingspan exceeds ~1.5 m.

Wing loading vs. stall:
  Higher W/S means a smaller, lighter wing and less drag. The stall
  constraint (Vs <= 12 m/s) puts a hard ceiling on W/S for a given CLmax.
  The optimizer always pushes W/S to that ceiling — the optimal design
  sits right at the stall constraint boundary at W/S ≈ 86-88 N/m².
  This is a classic constraint-active result.

Static margin vs. trim drag:
  Higher SM means the CG is further forward, requiring more trailing-edge-up
  elevon deflection to trim, which creates additional drag. Lower SM saves
  energy but risks instability. The optimizer targets SM ≈ 7.5% (midpoint
  of the 5-13% soft penalty band) — this is the quantified energy-versus-
  stability tradeoff embedded in the soft penalty coefficients.

Washout vs. L/D:
  More washout (larger negative twist) protects against tip stall and
  satisfies the flying-wing stability requirement (Cmα < 0). But washout
  reduces tip lift, so the wing must fly at a higher root angle of attack
  for the same total lift, increasing induced drag. The optimizer chose
  -2.33 degrees — the minimum needed to satisfy the SM constraint via
  the Panknin method. Any less and SM falls below 5%.

Payload volume vs. fuselage weight:
  The scoring function strongly rewards larger cargo bays. The optimizer
  always maximizes fuselage half-width (0.200 m) and pushes fuselage
  length to 1.0 m. Every physically achievable cargo bay improvement
  is worth taking, given the $300/m³ revenue coefficient.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  9. CONVERGENCE — HOW WE KNOW THE OPTIMIZER IS DONE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Two stopping criteria are checked each generation:

  1. Step size convergence: sigma < 1e-5
     The Gaussian distribution has collapsed to a single point. Every sample
     lands essentially at the same location. The optimizer has found a local
     basin and cannot meaningfully explore further.

  2. Objective plateau: range of best J over last 10 generations < 1e-6 $/s
     The landscape is flat enough that further iterations produce no improvement.

In practice, convergence happens around generation 200-300 with sigma dropping
from the initial 0.10 to below 1e-3 steadily. The convergence plot produced
at the end of the run shows sigma vs. generation — a steady exponential decay
indicates healthy convergence. A plateau followed by a drop indicates the
optimizer escaped a local optimum and found a better basin.

Maximum limit: 500 generations (~7 hours). If neither stopping criterion
triggers, the best solution found by generation 500 is returned.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  10. ONE-SENTENCE SUMMARY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

We use CMA-ES because the profit landscape is non-convex, multi-disciplinary,
and expensive to evaluate — gradient-based methods fail on non-smooth constraint
boundaries, genetic algorithms waste too many evaluations on a 7-hour budget,
and CMA-ES uniquely learns the shape of the 13-dimensional search space as it
runs, converging reliably in ~13,000 evaluations while enforcing flight safety
through a two-tier system of hard geometric and performance cutoffs combined
with soft quadratic stability penalties calibrated to MIL-STD-1797 handling
quality requirements.
